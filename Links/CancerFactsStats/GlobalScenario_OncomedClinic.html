Global Scenario of Cancer Burden

The global cancer burden is estimated to have risen to 18.1 million new cases and 9.6 million deaths in last year. One in 5 men and one in 6 women worldwide develop cancer during their lifetime, and one in 8 men and one in 11 women die from the disease. Worldwide, the total number of people who are alive within 5 years of a cancer diagnosis, called the 5-year prevalence, is estimated to be 43.8 million.
Cancers of the lung, female breast, and colo rectum are the top three cancer types across the globe. Europe accounts for 23.4% of the global cancer cases and 20.3% of the cancer deaths, whereas America have 21.0% of incidence and 14.4% of mortality worldwide. In contrast to other world regions, the proportions of cancer deaths in Asia and in Africa (57.3% and 7.3%, respectively) are higher. Approximately 70% of deaths from cancer occur in low- and middle-income countries.
The increasing cancer burden is due to several factors, including population growth and ageing as well as the changing prevalence of certain causes of cancer linked to social and economic development. This is particularly true in rapidly growing economies, where a shift is observed from cancers related to infections to cancers associated with lifestyles more typical of industrialized countries. Tobacco use is the most important risk factor for cancer and is responsible for approximately 22% of cancer deaths. Cancer causing infections, such as hepatitis and human papilloma virus (HPV), are responsible for up to 25% of cancer cases in low- and middle-income countries. The economic impact of cancer is significant and is increasing. The total annual economic cost of cancer in 2010 was estimated at approximately US$ 1.16 trillion.
The GLOBOCAN 2018 database, accessible online as part of the IARC Global Cancer Observatory, provides estimates of incidence and mortality in 185 countries for 36 types of cancer and for all cancer sites combined.
