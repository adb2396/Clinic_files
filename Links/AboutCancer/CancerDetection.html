Detection

Many approaches, techniques and terminology are used once you are suspected to have a cancer. These tests are done under the guidance of the oncologist/cancer specialist to prove or disapprove the diagnosis.

  • Lab Tests
  • Pathology
  • Imaging

There are certain proteins which are excreted by tumours in the blood and can be detected by a simple lab test. These tests are called as tumour markers . Some common ones are CA-125 for ovarian cancer, CEA for colon cancer and Ca 15-3 for breast cancer.

  • FNAC (Fine needle aspiration and cytology): It is a simple , quick OPD procedure in which a needle is used to collect sample from lumps anywhere in the body. Multiple punctures by a very thin needle collect some cells which are fixed on a glass slide and studied by the pathologist in lab.
  • BIOPSY : A big bore needle is inserted either directly or under the guidance of CT scan / Ultrasound and multiple cores of tumour tissue are collected. These bits are then either spread on a slide by taking very thin sections or are also preserved in paraffin blocks. These are then stained and studied by pathologist.
  • IMMUNOHISTOCHEMISTRY: Once it is confirmed as cancer then we apply special stains called as IHC (Immuno histochemistry) on unstained slides/ biopsy or cell blocks. These stains help us to know the type of cancer as well in some cases origin of the tumour.
  • Ultrasound : An ultrasound device sends out sound waves/echoes and computer uses these echoes to create a picture of areas inside your body. This picture is called a sonogram. It is very helpful to study breast lumps in young females or lymph nodes on neck.
  • CT Scan : An x-ray machine linked to a computer takes a series of detailed pictures of your organs. You may receive a dye or other contrast material to highlight areas inside the body. Contrast material helps make these pictures easier to read. CT scan are very helpful to study lung tumors, liver tumors and various other organs in the abdomen.
  • MRI : A strong magnet linked to a computer is used to make detailed pictures of areas in your body. Your doctor can view these pictures on a monitor and print them on film.It is the imaging modality of choice to study brain cancers and bone cancers.
  • PET scan : For this scan, you receive an injection of a radionuclide tracer dye. Then, a CT scan machine makes 3-D pictures of the whole body that show where the tracer collects in the body. The most common tracer used is FDG glucose. The tumor or infection in the whole body shows high uptake of FDG glucose. This scan is done fasting and for diabetic patients fasting blood sugar shall be less than 180 mg/dl. It usually takes around 4 hours for the complete study. The most common PET used in oncology is 18 FDG PET SCAN. Other PET scan are specific for the tumour type such as PSMA scan for prostate cancer and DOTA scan for neuroendocrine tumours.
